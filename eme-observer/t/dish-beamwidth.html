Explanation & Formulas

Half-Power Beamwidth (HPBW, 3dB) approximation:

HPBW ≈ 70 × (λ / D)

where λ is the wavelength and D is the dish diameter. This approximation is valid for typical parabolic dishes with ~60% efficiency. The efficiency of a real-world antenna is affected by the geometric properties of the reflector and feeding system (dish surface accuracy, illumination, feed blockage, etc).

Beamwidth at an arbitrary (X) Beamwidth Level:

Beamwidth at X dB ≈ HPBW × √(X / 3)

where X is the corresponding Beamwidth Level (for instance, 1dB, 0.5dB or 0.1dB)

These values are exact by definition of the contours. In practice, remaining well within the 0.5 dB or 0.1 dB beamwidth on lower EME bands, and within 0.05 dB or 0.1 dB on higher EME bands, ensures negligible loss.

Practical Limit: Electrically Small Aperture

For a given frequency, an antenna becomes electrically small when the dish diameter D ≈ 2λ. In this region, the corresponding beamwidth becomes very large (on the order of ~30–40°). The orange dashed line at ~35° marks this practical limit.

Above this beamwidth (i.e., for electrically small antennas), diffraction effects dominate, and the antenna loses directivity and gain, behaving more like a weakly directive or quasi-omnidirectional radiator.

In practice, even approaching this limit is problematic: the feed system becomes strongly coupled to the reflector, blockage effects increase, and the dish no longer forms an efficient aperture.
